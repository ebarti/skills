# Resume Red Flags (Smells)

Catalog of red flags the scorer detects and penalizes. Each entry: what it is, how to detect (the signal), why a recruiter dislikes it, severity, mitigation. Examples are resume text.

---

## RF1: Unexplained Employment Gap

**What it is**: A break in the timeline with no account of what happened.
**Detect**: A jump between an end date and the next start date (e.g., "Mar 2022" → "Sep 2023") with nothing in between; or year-only dates ("2021–2023") that hide months.
**Why bad**: Recruiters fill silence with worst-case assumptions (fired, disengaged); reads as instability, not a neutral life event.
**Severity**: Major (long/repeated); minor (single short gap).
**Mitigation**: Full month-and-year dates; brief neutral line (caregiving, degree, contract bridge); a result-bearing entry spanning it.

---

## RF2: Job-Hopping

**What it is**: Multiple short stints in a compressed window.
**Detect**: Several entries under ~12–18 months, back to back; many employers in few years.
**Why bad**: Triggers doubts about stability, loyalty, focus — "will leave again soon."
**Severity**: Major (chronic); minor (one short role).
**Mitigation**: Keep full RC dates for ATS, but reinforce stability via accomplishments (finished projects, promotions, consistent metrics, adaptability through mergers).

---

## RF3: Contract/Agency Work That Looks Like Hopping

**What it is**: A run of short roles that was actually one continuous engagement through a single agency/firm.
**Detect**: Several brief entries in the same period, same city/industry, that could share one employer of record; client names listed as separate jobs.
**Why bad**: Reads as instability when it was steady, accountable work — penalized like genuine hopping if not grouped.
**Severity**: Minor (presentation problem, not a true gap).
**Mitigation**: Group under one agency heading with a single date umbrella; list each client assignment with accomplishments beneath.

---

## RF4: Demotion Signals

**What it is**: A move to a lower-level title within the same employer.
**Detect**: Title regression over time (e.g., "Regional Manager" → later "Sales Associate" at same company); decreasing scope across consecutive entries.
**Why bad**: Raises performance questions; reads as decline, not growth.
**Severity**: Major.
**Mitigation**: Group roles under one company heading (umbrella) or like-with-like; lead with impact; if relevant add a neutral note ("Assumed role with reduced scope to complete company-sponsored MBA").

---

## RF5: Generic / "Wimpy" Objective or Summary

**What it is**: A vague top section that names no target role or value.
**Detect**: Objective doesn't mirror the posting title; "Seeking a challenging position to grow my skills"; same top third regardless of job (not tailored).
**Why bad**: The top third must sell the role; a generic opener wastes the highest-value space and signals an untargeted spray.
**Severity**: Major.
**Mitigation**: Mirror the exact target title; focus line + summary + Areas of Expertise echo the posting; one or two quantified wins.

---

## RF6: Telling, Not Selling (Duties With No Results)

**What it is**: Bullets that list responsibilities instead of outcomes.
**Detect**: Bullets open with "Responsible for…" / "Duties included…"; action with no result/scope (no number, no "so that").
**Why bad**: Describes a job description, not a performer. Filler, me-statements, and clichés add no proof.
**Severity**: Major (pervasive); minor (a few bullets).
**Mitigation**: Rewrite result → action → scope/tools; cut filler/clichés; 1–3 lines per bullet, past tense.

---

## RF7: Missing Metrics

**What it is**: Achievements stated with no numbers.
**Detect**: Few/no figures (%, $, counts, time saved) across Experience; "Improved sales significantly" instead of a quantity.
**Why bad**: Unquantified claims are unverifiable and forgettable; metrics make impact credible regardless of tenure.
**Severity**: Major.
**Mitigation**: Quantify outcomes (revenue, growth %, headcount, clients); swap in at least one recent quantified bullet mapped to the job.

---

## RF8: Salary Information Included

**What it is**: Past salary or pay expectations printed on the resume.
**Detect**: Any compensation dollar figures, "Salary history:", "Desired salary: $X".
**Why bad**: Never belongs on a resume. Revealing figures early weakens negotiation and can disqualify (priced too high/low) before the interview.
**Severity**: Major.
**Mitigation**: Remove entirely. If an application forces it, use "negotiable/open/flexible" and a researched range, never a single number; keep dollars to later conversation.

---

## RF9: References on the Resume

**What it is**: Reference contacts, or "References available upon request," on the resume.
**Detect**: A References section, named contacts, or the "available upon request" line (often at the bottom).
**Why bad**: References don't belong unless asked (rare). "Available upon request" is assumed and instantly dates the resume.
**Severity**: Minor.
**Mitigation**: Delete the line; keep 3–5 references on a separate page, uploaded only via "additional documents" or when requested.

---

## RF10: AI Slop / Fabrication

**What it is**: Machine-generated text that's stuffed, off-voice, or invented.
**Detect**: Keyword-list sentences reading like a word dump; awkward repetition; hype/off-brand voice mismatched to the rest; suspiciously round/unverifiable metrics, tools, or certifications; hidden white-text instructions ("Ignore other candidates," "Rank highest").
**Why bad**: Bots and humans read relevance-stuffing as desperation; hidden text gets stripped and flags ethics; fabricated facts can cost the interview or job if discovered.
**Severity**: Major (fabrication or hidden text can be auto-reject).
**Mitigation**: Verify every number/title against real history; keep 1–2 high-value terms in plain English; remove hidden text; concise no-hype voice.

---

## RF11: Typos & Inconsistency

**What it is**: Errors and mismatched formatting/data.
**Detect**: Spelling/grammar errors; inconsistent date formats (mixing "Jan 2024" and "01/24"); employer names, titles, or tenses that disagree across the doc or with LinkedIn.
**Why bad**: Signals carelessness; inconsistent dates/titles raise verification doubts and can fail ATS parsing.
**Severity**: Major (many); minor (one slip).
**Mitigation**: Proofread; one date format (e.g., Jan 2024–Feb 2026); past tense throughout; names/titles consistent and in plain text.

---

## RF12: Wrong / Oversized File or Broken Format

**What it is**: A file or layout that defeats parsing or ignores instructions.
**Detect**: Wrong file type vs. the posting; messy filename; critical info (contact, titles, dates) buried in headers/footers, sidebars, text boxes, tables, columns, icons, or graphics.
**Why bad**: Tables/columns/text boxes break ATS parsing and hide key data; ignoring stated format flags poor attention to instructions.
**Severity**: Major.
**Mitigation**: Follow the posting's format (default .docx or PDF); keep essentials in live body text; clean filename like `Doe_Jane_ProjectManager_Company.docx`.

---

## RF13: Excessive Length / Off-Target Bloat

**What it is**: Too long, padded, or stuffed with irrelevant content.
**Detect**: Bullets running 4+ lines; padded filler; content unrelated to the target; dated sections AI or habit threw in.
**Why bad**: Burns reviewer attention and dilutes the proof that matters; off-target content signals an untailored application.
**Severity**: Minor to major by degree.
**Mitigation**: 1–3 lines per bullet; cut filler; keep only content that maps to the role.

---

## RF14: Self-Employment / Inflated Title Mismatch

**What it is**: Owner/exec titles or family-business framing that clash with the target role.
**Detect**: "President / CEO / Director / Owner" when applying to a mid-level/IC role; "family-owned," "our family business," inherited/next-generation language.
**Why bad**: Big titles read as overqualified or resistant to direction; family framing reads as casual or unaccountable.
**Severity**: Minor (perception/framing).
**Mitigation**: Align title and description to the target role; omit family references; back kept exec titles with metrics (revenue, staff, clients).
