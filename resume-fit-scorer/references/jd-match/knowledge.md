# JD-Match Knowledge

Core concepts for judging how well a resume matches a job description (JD) and whether it survives automated screening plus a recruiter's first glance.

## Overview

A resume rarely fails because the candidate is unqualified. It fails because it does not *demonstrate* the match in a way that survives three sequential screens: an ATS parser, a 6-10 second human skim, and a decision-maker's close read. JD-match measures how well the resume's language, keywords, titles, and proof line up with the posting at each stage.

## The 3 Screening Stages

A resume travels this path in roughly twenty seconds. It must pass each gate to reach the next.

### Stage 1 — ATS Filters / Bots / Keyword Parsing

**Definition**: An Applicant Tracking System (ATS) is a database that ingests resumes, *parses* them (reads and maps content into fields: name, title, employer, dates, education, skills), and compares them to the posting. Only strong matches reach a human.

**Key points**:
- The system reads titles, employers, dates, and skills, then scores relevance against the JD.
- Parsing maps your text into structured boxes; poor structure causes misreads or dropped data.
- Some systems strip layout, bullets, and styling. Info in sidebars, tables, headers/footers, or text boxes can vanish.
- Pasted content can lose characters (bullets, smart quotes, em dashes) or collapse line breaks.
- The goal is to *work with* the system, not "beat" it. Keyword tricks (stuffing, invisible text) backfire with both bots and humans.

### Stage 2 — The ~6-Second Recruiter First Glance

**Definition**: A busy screener (rarely the hiring manager) gives each resume a 6-10 second look for *obvious* fit. Clear match makes the "maybe" pile; otherwise it is out.

**Key points**:
- The decision is driven almost entirely by the **top third** of page one.
- Screener looks for: role match, aligned skills, and quick wins (results).
- Common stalls: language mismatch, top third does not signal role/skills/wins, or Experience lacks keyword proof.
- The resume must *sell* a unique brand and value, not merely list qualifications.

### Stage 3 — The Decision-Maker Read

**Definition**: If the resume reaches the hiring manager, the bar rises. Prior screening already implies fit; now they confirm the match *and* what makes the candidate stand out enough to be worth an interview.

**Key points**:
- Every section is read on purpose: focus line mirrors title, summary connects scope to their priorities with proof, Experience leads with results that map to key requirements.
- Differentiation matters: story-based bullets (challenge → action → result) ordered by relevance.
- Education/training included only when it advances candidacy for the target role.

## What Employers Crave

Employers want **impact, reliability, and alignment**. A resume wins when it translates the candidate's work into the employer's outcomes: growth, savings, speed, quality, safety, or satisfaction. Front-loaded bullets that begin with an outcome (not a duty) "sell, not just tell," and make the employer's scanning job easy.

## Keyword / Requirement Coverage

**Definition**: The degree to which the JD's required skills, titles, and terminology appear — truthfully — in the resume, especially in the top third and Experience section.

**Key points**:
- **Coverage** = how many of the JD's must-haves are present and proven.
- **Mirrored terminology** = using the posting's *exact* vocabulary ("translate, don't invent").
- **Keyword proof** = the Experience section backs up listed skills with evidence, not just an Areas-of-Expertise list.
- Acronyms are spelled out on first use so both bots and humans match them.

## Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| ATS | Applicant Tracking System: database that collects, parses, and ranks resumes vs. the posting |
| Parsing | System reading a resume and mapping content into fields (name, title, dates, skills) |
| OnTarget resume | A core resume tailored to one specific role/employer (focus line, summary, keywords, proof aligned to the JD) |
| Focus line | Top-of-resume line naming the target role; mirrors the JD title (not an old "Objective" sentence) |
| Branding summary | 3-4 lines signaling what you do, at what scope, and the value/results you bring |
| Areas of expertise | Keyword list (often 3 columns) of the specific skills the position requires |
| Crossover / mirrored language | Reusing the posting's exact vocabulary so resume and JD speak the same terms |
| Top third | The upper portion of page one that drives the 6-second glance decision |
| First glance | The 6-10 second human skim for obvious fit |
| Keyword proof | Evidence in Experience that substantiates the skills you claim |
| Keyword stuffing | Cramming/hiding keywords to game the ATS; backfires with humans and filters |

## Common Misconceptions

- **Myth**: A strong generic resume works everywhere.
  **Reality**: Generic resumes "spray and pray" — vague, missing keywords, failing the first-glance test. Each application needs an OnTarget version.

- **Myth**: Loading the resume with keywords beats the ATS.
  **Reality**: Stuffing or invisible text backfires with humans and can be filtered by systems. Use exact terms truthfully and prove them.

- **Myth**: Being qualified is enough.
  **Reality**: You must *show* the match quickly and stand out from equally qualified competition.

## Quick Reference

| Stage | Gatekeeper | Looks For | Fails When |
|-------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| 1. ATS parse | Software | Title/employer/date/skill match | Unparseable layout, no keyword overlap |
| 2. First glance | Recruiter (6-10s) | Role match, skills, wins in top third | Top third doesn't signal fit; language mismatch |
| 3. Decision-maker | Hiring manager | Proof + differentiation per section | No results mapping to key requirements |
