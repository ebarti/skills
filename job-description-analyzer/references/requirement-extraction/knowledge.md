# Requirement Extraction Knowledge

Core concepts for reading a job description (JD) and pulling out structured requirements.

## Overview

A job description encodes what an employer "craves": impact, reliability, and alignment. Hiring teams and their tools (ATS) judge fit by the exact words used. Requirement extraction means parsing the raw JD into a structured set of requirements — split by priority (must-have vs nice-to-have), by type (hard vs soft skill), plus responsibilities and a read on the target seniority.

## Anatomy of a Job Description

A typical JD is a mix of sections. Only some carry extractable requirements.

| Section | What it contains | Extract? |
|---------|------------------|----------|
| Title + summary | Role name, one-line mission, level cue | Yes (seniority) |
| Responsibilities / duties | What the person will DO day-to-day | Yes |
| Requirements / qualifications | What the candidate must HAVE | Yes (must/nice) |
| Preferred / nice-to-have | Bonus qualifications | Yes (nice) |
| Skills / competencies | Tools, methods, traits | Yes (hard/soft) |
| Company blurb, benefits, EEO | Boilerplate, perks, legal | No (ignore) |

## Key Concepts

### Must-Have (Required) Requirements

**Definition**: Qualifications the candidate is required to possess to be considered for the role.

These are non-negotiable. Marked by hard language ("required", "must", "minimum", "X+ years"). Failing many of these usually means the resume is screened out.

**Key points**:
- Apply for roles where you meet the MAJORITY of must-haves.
- These map first when checking fit; they anchor the keyword section.

### Nice-to-Have (Preferred) Requirements

**Definition**: Qualifications that strengthen a candidate but are not strictly required.

Marked by soft language ("preferred", "bonus", "a plus", "nice to have"). Gaps here can be covered with adjacent or crossover experience.

**Key points**:
- Aim for ~70-80% of nice-to-haves, or adjacent/crossover proof.
- Lower weight than must-haves when assessing fit.

### Hard Skills

**Definition** (from the book): The technical qualifications you possess that your profession and target position require.

Each role has a specific, often unique set (e.g., a veterinary technician: animal restraint techniques, medication dosage calculation, IV catheter placement, X-rays, blood draws, lab testing, diagnostic equipment operation). Hard skills are concrete and measurable; they become the cornerstone of a resume's keyword section.

**Key points**:
- Includes tools, certifications/licenses, and measurable competencies.
- Worded differently per employer/industry — mirror the JD's exact phrasing.
- A JD typically yields enough for 9-21 key skills/competencies.

### Soft Skills

**Definition** (from the book): Interpersonal talents such as the ability to work as part of a team or to communicate effectively with customers.

Often implied or assumed for the role. Because they are generic, they are harder to "prove" with a keyword — they are shown through accomplishments rather than listed.

**Key points**:
- Examples: teamwork, communication, leadership, adaptability.
- Generic by nature; extract them but tag separately from hard skills.

### Responsibilities / Duties

**Definition**: What the person in the role will DO — the day-to-day work and scope of ownership.

Usually action-led ("Lead...", "Build...", "Own..."). The verbs and breadth of ownership are the strongest signal of seniority.

### Qualifications

**Definition**: The bundle of requirements (education, experience, skills, certs) used to gate candidates.

Split into required (must-have) and preferred (nice-to-have) buckets during extraction.

### Seniority / Scope Signals

**Definition**: Cues in the JD that indicate the target level and breadth of the role.

**Key points**:
- Years of experience ("2+ years" = junior/mid; "8+ years" = senior/lead).
- Verbs of scope ("assist/support" → junior; "own/lead/define strategy" → senior).
- People scope (mentoring, managing, hiring → lead/manager).
- Title modifiers (Junior, Senior, Staff, Principal, Lead, Head, Director).

## Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| JD | Job description / job posting |
| Must-have | Required qualification (non-negotiable) |
| Nice-to-have | Preferred qualification (bonus) |
| Hard skill | Technical, measurable qualification (tools, certs) |
| Soft skill | Interpersonal talent (teamwork, communication) |
| Competency | A demonstrated ability combining skill + behavior |
| Responsibility | A duty the role will perform |
| Qualification | A requirement gating the candidate |
| Seniority signal | Cue indicating target level/scope |
| ATS | Applicant Tracking System that scans resumes by keyword |
| Crossover term | Wording that maps adjacent experience onto a requirement |
| Boilerplate | Non-requirement filler (EEO, benefits, company blurb) |

## How It Relates To

- **Keyword & crossover extraction**: Extracted hard skills + must-haves feed the keyword list; nice-to-have gaps are bridged with crossover terms.
- **Fit assessment**: Structured must/nice lists let JobHunter score candidate fit (majority of must-haves, 70-80% of nice-to-haves).

## Common Misconceptions

- **Myth**: Every line in a JD is a requirement.
  **Reality**: Benefits, EEO statements, and company history are boilerplate — ignore them.

- **Myth**: Soft skills belong in the keyword list.
  **Reality**: They are too generic to list as keywords; capture them but show them through accomplishments.

- **Myth**: A skill listed under "preferred" is optional fluff.
  **Reality**: It is a real signal — just weighted lower than a must-have.
