# Keywords & Crossover Knowledge

Core concepts for pulling the right keywords from a job description (JD) and mapping them to the candidate's own vocabulary.

## Overview

A JD signals what the employer wants through specific words and phrasing. Two extraction jobs follow from this: (1) harvest the high-value **ATS keywords** the candidate must mirror, and (2) build a **crossover map** that translates the candidate's real experience into the JD's terms when the two speak "different languages." The goal is alignment, not duplication — mirror truthfully, never stuff or clone.

## Key Concepts

### ATS Keywords

**Definition**: The exact high-value terms an employer uses in a posting — job titles, tools, skills, certifications, and valued outcomes — that applicant tracking software (ATS) and human screeners match against.

The employer "spoon-feeds" what they want via these words. Software often prefers **exact matches** over synonyms, and busy screeners may miss synonyms entirely.

**Key points**:
- Group as **must-have** vs. **nice-to-have**.
- Capture titles, skills/tools, requirements, certifications/licenses, and outcome terms (quota, retention, uptime).
- ATS also weighs *how* a term is used: listing "CRM" helps; proving it lifted retention helps more.

### Crossover Language

**Definition**: Relaying the message of a resume in the *employer's* vocabulary — a bridge that translates the candidate's past work into the target field's terms.

If the employer speaks healthcare and the candidate speaks engineering, the experience won't register until it's restated in the reader's words (e.g., cabinetry phrasing → project-management phrasing).

**Key points**:
- Translates wording **without changing the facts**.
- Most valuable when the fit isn't perfect but the experience is genuinely adjacent.
- Only use language the candidate can defend in an interview.

### Crossover Map

**Definition**: A two-column mapping of `their_term ↔ your_equivalent` — the JD's wording on one side, the candidate's truthful equivalent on the other.

Built only where the candidate genuinely has the equivalent experience. Drives the rewrite of the resume's "Areas of Expertise" and "Experience" sections.

**Key points**:
- One row per requirement: their term → candidate's proof → suggested wording.
- Asymmetric is fine — not every JD term has an equivalent; leave gaps honest.

### Mirroring the Ad ("To Clone or Not to Clone")

**Definition**: Reusing the posting's exact words **whenever they truthfully fit**, while keeping the language flowing organically rather than copy-pasting the ad.

Mirror, don't clone. Keywords belong in two places: the top "Areas of Expertise" block and the "Experience" bullets that prove them.

**Key points**:
- Exact matches beat synonyms for hard requirements.
- Make terms flow naturally — do not dump or stack them.

### Over-Cloning / Keyword Stuffing

**Definition**: Cramming JD terms into the resume unnaturally, or copying the ad wholesale, including claims the candidate can't back up.

The failure mode of mirroring taken too far: unreadable, undefendable, and easy for screeners to spot.

## Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| ATS | Applicant tracking system; software that parses and matches resumes to a JD |
| ATS keyword | High-value JD term (title/tool/skill/cert/outcome) to mirror |
| Must-have | Required keyword the candidate should match exactly |
| Nice-to-have | Preferred keyword; cover ~70–80% with adjacent/crossover proof |
| Crossover language | Restating experience in the employer's vocabulary, facts unchanged |
| Crossover map | `their_term ↔ your_equivalent` table built only on truthful equivalents |
| Mirroring | Reusing the posting's exact words where they truthfully fit |
| Over-cloning | Copying the ad wholesale or stuffing terms unnaturally |
| OnTarget | A resume curated from the candidate's core to speak one employer's language |
| Exact match | Verbatim term ATS/screeners prefer over a synonym |

## How It Relates To

- **Resume tailoring (OnTarget)**: Keywords and crossover terms feed the rewrite of the objective header, summary, and Areas of Expertise.
- **Fit assessment**: The crossover map reveals match strength — apply when most must-haves and 70–80% of nice-to-haves are covered.
- **Section structure**: Keywords land in "Areas of Expertise" (list) and are proven in "Experience" (bullets).

## Common Misconceptions

- **Myth**: Synonyms are good enough for ATS.
  **Reality**: Software often prefers exact matches and screeners may miss synonyms — mirror the exact term.

- **Myth**: More keywords = higher ranking.
  **Reality**: Stuffing reads poorly and ATS weighs *usage*; proof of impact beats raw repetition.

- **Myth**: Crossover language means rewriting your history to fit.
  **Reality**: It translates wording only; facts never change and every term must be interview-defensible.
