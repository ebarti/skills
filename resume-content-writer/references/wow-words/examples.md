## Weak → Strong Rewrite Examples

Each pair takes a limp, duty-style bullet and upgrades it with a wow verb, outcome-first
framing, and keyword nouns — per the chapter's guidance.

### 1. "Responsible for" → strong verb

- **Weak**: Responsible for the customer support email queue.
- **Strong**: Resolved 1,200+ monthly support tickets, cutting average response time 35%.

### 2. "Helped with" → results verb

- **Weak**: Helped with the company's social media accounts.
- **Strong**: Grew Instagram following 60% in six months by launching a weekly content series.

### 3. "Worked on" → technical build verb

- **Weak**: Worked on the new checkout feature.
- **Strong**: Engineered a one-click checkout flow that lifted conversion 12%.

### 4. "Duties included" → outcome-first verb

- **Weak**: Duties included managing vendor relationships and contracts.
- **Strong**: Negotiated 15 vendor contracts, saving $80K annually.

### 5. "Was in charge of" → leadership verb

- **Weak**: Was in charge of the regional sales team.
- **Strong**: Directed a 9-person regional sales team to 120% of annual quota.

### 6. "Assisted in" → analysis verb

- **Weak**: Assisted in analyzing quarterly performance data.
- **Strong**: Analyzed quarterly revenue data and identified $250K in upsell opportunities.

### 7. Flat process → revived/streamlined verb

- **Weak**: Responsible for the onboarding process.
- **Strong**: Streamlined new-hire onboarding, reducing ramp time from 6 weeks to 3.

## Pattern: How Each Rewrite Was Built

1. **Drop** the weak opener (pronoun/article/"responsible for") and any filler.
2. **Pick** a precise verb from the matching functional category above.
3. **Lead** with the outcome (number/result) where possible.
4. **Anchor** with keyword nouns that mirror the target job description.
5. **Keep** past tense and the facts unchanged — no embellishment.
