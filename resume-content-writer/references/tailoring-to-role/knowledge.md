# Tailoring to Role Knowledge

Core concepts for bending resume content toward one specific target job.

## Overview

A generic, one-size-fits-all resume is no longer competitive. The winning approach is to keep a master "Core" resume and curate a focused version for each target job, written in that employer's vocabulary. This is the "OnTarget" method: same true facts, re-aimed emphasis and language.

## Key Concepts

### OnTarget Resume

**Definition**: A version of the Core resume curated and reworded to match one specific job posting.

It is built by mining the job description for what the employer values, then surfacing the matching experience, skills, and keywords from the Core. The facts never change — only the emphasis, ordering, and wording.

**Key points**:
- Derived from a stable Core resume (the source of truth)
- One OnTarget version per target role; save each for reuse
- Tweak a saved version for similar future postings

### Crossover Language

**Definition**: Relaying the message of your resume in the employer's vocabulary — translating your past work into the terms of the target field.

Picture a bridge between your experience and the employer's world. If they speak "healthcare" and you speak "engineering," you must translate so your value registers. Example: home-cabinetry phrasing reframed as project-management terminology.

**Key points**:
- Translate, don't fabricate — the underlying work must be real
- Re-label people, work, and outcomes in the target's terms
- Only use language you can defend in an interview

### Mirroring the Ad

**Definition**: Reusing the exact words from the posting whenever they truthfully fit.

ATS software often prefers exact matches over synonyms, and busy human screeners may miss synonyms. Mirroring the job title in your objective header and reusing posted skill terms raises both machine and human match rates.

**Key points**:
- Exact matches beat synonyms for ATS and skimmers
- ATS also checks *how* a term was used — prove impact, don't just list
- Keep keyword nouns in plain text at least once (parsers strip styling)

### Featuring Relevant & Recent Experience First

**Definition**: Putting the most effort and top placement on the experience that best proves the target role.

If the most recent job is the most relevant, lead with it. If not, find what is most relevant and put your energy (and top bullets) there. Older/less relevant roles get progressively less detail.

**Key points**:
- Lead each role with its strongest, most on-target bullets
- Limit to ~4 positions or 10–15 years of history
- Other experience (volunteer, internships, projects) counts when relevant

### Skills Section (Areas of Expertise)

**Definition**: A near-top list (1–3 columns, ~9–21 bullets) of the key skills/keywords for the role, drawn straight from the posting.

It is one of two homes for keywords (the other is Experience). The skills you list must then be *proven* in the Experience section.

**Key points**:
- Use the posting's exact terms, ordered by the posting's priorities
- Listing a skill helps; proving it drove an outcome helps more
- Select and reorder per target — this section is highly tailorable

## Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Core resume | Master source document; superset of all true content |
| OnTarget resume | Core curated/reworded for one specific posting |
| Crossover language | Restating your work in the target field's vocabulary |
| Mirroring | Reusing the ad's exact words where truthful |
| Objective header | Top title line; should mirror the posted job title |
| Areas of Expertise | The keyword skills section near the top |
| CAR statement | Challenge–Action–Result accomplishment bullet |
| Keyword stuffing | Cramming terms unnaturally; an over-cloning anti-pattern |

## How It Relates To

- **ATS optimization**: Mirroring exact terms is what parsers reward; plain-text nouns survive parsing.
- **Summary / USP**: The top third is rewritten per target using the values mined from the posting.
- **AI-assisted writing**: AI prompts extract posting terms and suggest accurate crossover phrasing.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Myth**: One strong generic resume works for every application.
  **Reality**: Generic only fits rare cases (job fairs, single-niche focus, networking). Targeted is the norm.

- **Myth**: Cramming every keyword from the ad maximizes matches.
  **Reality**: Stuffing reads as spam and fails the human skim; terms must flow organically and be provable.

- **Myth**: Crossover language means rewording until it matches.
  **Reality**: It translates real work into new terms; it never invents experience you lack.
