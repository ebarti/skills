# Headline & Branding Summary Rules

Rules for writing a headline and branding summary that open a resume and win the interview.

## Core Rules

### 1. Lead with the target job title

State the specific role as a header; echo the posting's title verbatim when appropriate.

- Drop generic, long-winded objective sentences — get right to the role.
- Use a literal focus line: `[Target Title] — [primary scope/industry/edge]`.
- Example focus line: `Product Marketing Manager — B2B SaaS, PLG, GTM Launches.`

### 2. State a quantified value proposition (ROI)

Sell the return on investment: faster, better, more profitable/efficient, or risk-reduced.

- Lead the summary's claims with outcomes and metrics where accurate (%, $, time saved).
- Build the value from CAR stories (Challenge, Action, Result) and unique credentials.
- An optional proof line starts with the outcome, then names the lever: e.g. "cut onboarding time 43% by standardizing CRM workflows across 3 regions."

### 3. Tailor to the job description (JD)

Make every summary OnTarget — tight and dynamic toward the specific posting.

- Weave in 3-6 of the most relevant areas of expertise, naturally, not as a list.
- Use the employer's own language and keywords (keywords win relevance).
- Choose emphasis by JD lean: market/financial impact (revenue, growth, CAC/LTV, share) = strategic; execution/throughput/quality (SLAs, uptime, cycle time, defects) = operational.

### 4. Keep it tight (length guidance)

- Branding summary: **3-5 lines**, one or two short paragraphs, a bullet list, or a blend.
- Tighten a long draft to **≤ 67 words** while preserving metrics, scope, and keywords.
- Don't oversell: irrelevant "extra" data makes you look overqualified or confused.

### 5. Avoid first-person fluff and generic clichés

- Cut filler traits: "good communicator," "works well with others," "results-oriented."
- Avoid buzzy fluff and anything you cannot prove.
- Replace vague claims with concise proof (a number or a named result).

### 6. Keep it ATS-clean and honest

- Plain text, no icons or graphics, so applicant tracking systems can parse it.
- Never invent numbers, brands, or credentials. If a line isn't provably yours, cut it.

## Guidelines

- A credential belongs in the summary only if it's unique or required by the posting; common ones go in the education section.
- Generate strategic and operational variants and save both for future similar postings.
- You may blend variants — steal one strong line from the other if total length and proof hold.
- The summary is a teaser; do not try to prove every claim here — proof lives in experience.

## AI-Prompting Tips for Summaries

Inputs needed: target job posting, your areas of expertise, and the experience section of your core resume.

1. **Draft results-first, ATS-clean**: ask the AI (as a "Master resume writer") to start with the focus line, then 3-5 ROI lines using employer language and metrics, weaving in 3-6 areas of expertise naturally; avoid clichés and unprovable claims; plain text.
2. **Generate variants**: produce a Strategic (market/financial-forward) and an Operational (execution/quality-forward) version, same length, no new facts; pick by JD lean.
3. **Tighten**: reduce to ≤ 67 words, preserving metrics/scope/keywords and the focus line.
4. **Add proof (optional)**: one outcome-first line (number/% → lever).
5. **Add a variant (optional)**: a student/career-changer version selling transferable ROI from projects/volunteer work/certs; stay honest about scope.

## Exceptions

- **Core (one-size-fits-all) resume**: this section may hold all your strengths; tighten it only when going OnTarget.
- **Early career / pivot**: scope can come from projects, volunteer work, and certs instead of job metrics — keep claims honest.
