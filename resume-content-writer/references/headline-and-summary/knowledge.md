# Headline & Branding Summary Knowledge

Core concepts for the two elements that open a resume: the headline/objective and the branding summary.

## Overview

The top third of a resume is its entrance. Two elements live there: a **headline** (the target job title) and a **branding summary** (3-5 lines explaining why this candidate beats equally qualified competition). Together they form a 30-second commercial that must be tailored to the specific job being targeted.

## Key Concepts

### Headline / Objective

**Definition**: A header stating the specific role the candidate is targeting — ideally the exact title from the job posting.

It sets context for the summary and skills that follow. It is NOT a long-winded "I want to grow my career" sentence; it is the role name.

**Key points**:
- Echo the posting's job title verbatim when appropriate, so the employer instantly sees a fit.
- Without it, the employer doesn't know which role is being applied for and the resume gets discarded.
- Can carry a literal "focus line" format: `[Target Title] — [primary scope/industry/edge]`.

### Branding Summary

**Definition**: A 3-5 line section that supports the headline's claim by highlighting why this candidate is the best fit out of other qualified applicants.

It signals **role, scope, and edge**. It is a teaser — proof comes later in the experience section, not here. Typically one or two short paragraphs, a bullet list, or a combination.

**Key points**:
- Contains the 3-5 skills/competencies that best support the job aspiration.
- Sells a **return on investment**: making/saving money, improving quality/satisfaction, reducing risk.
- "Keywords win relevance, and hooks win persuasion."

### Opening Pitch

**Definition**: The 30-second commercial the summary delivers — why the candidate is a match worth pursuing.

For a generic core resume it holds all strengths; for an OnTarget (tailored) resume it must be made tight and dynamic toward the specific job.

### Value Snapshot (USP)

**Definition**: A condensed statement of the candidate's **unique selling proposition** — what makes them stand out, not just qualify.

Built from two sources: **CAR stories** (Challenge, Action, Result — turnarounds, cost savings, time savings) and **key selling features** (degrees, certifications, licenses, awards, publications) that differentiate.

## Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Headline / Objective | Header stating the targeted role/title |
| Branding summary | 3-5 line pitch on why you're the best fit |
| Focus line | Literal `Title — scope/industry/edge` opener |
| USP | Unique selling proposition; what sets you apart |
| CAR story | Challenge faced, Action taken, Result attained |
| Key selling feature | Credential/award that differentiates you |
| ROI line | Outcome-first proof line (number → lever) |
| OnTarget resume | A resume tailored to one specific posting |
| ATS-clean | Plain text, no icons/graphics, parseable |

**Summary aliases**: areas of expertise, skills summary, highlights summary, asset statement, power summary, career highlights, career summary, career profile, career focus, summary of qualifications, unique selling proposition, accomplishments profile.

## When to Use Objective vs Branding Summary

- **Both are used together** at the top: the objective declares the role, the summary defends the claim.
- The objective alone is never enough — it states the target but offers no proof of fit.
- The summary alone is risky without a headline — the employer may not know which role you want.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Myth**: A generic objective ("seeking a challenging role to grow my skills") is fine.
  **Reality**: Generic objectives are weak filler. State the specific role title instead.

- **Myth**: A summary of generic traits ("good communicator," "works well with others") sells you.
  **Reality**: Those phrases group you with the whole applicant pool. Sell your USP with metrics.

- **Myth**: More is better — cram every strength in to look impressive.
  **Reality**: Overselling with irrelevant data makes you look overqualified or confused; no second look.

- **Myth**: Claims in the summary must be proven right there.
  **Reality**: The summary is a teaser; proof lives in the experience section. Keep it tight.

- **Myth**: Every credential belongs in the summary.
  **Reality**: A credential earns summary space only if it's unique/required; common ones (e.g., an unrequested MBA) go in the education section.
