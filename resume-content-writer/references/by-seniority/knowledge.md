# Framing Achievements by Seniority Knowledge

Core model for how achievement framing and content emphasis shift across seniority levels.

## Overview

SENIORITY is the primary lever for tailoring resume content. The same person needs different framing at different career stages: a new grad must *manufacture* evidence of workplace readiness from non-paid sources, while an experienced candidate must *curate* a long history down to recent, relevant, quantified impact. The goal at every level is the same — prove value and ROI to the employer — but the source of proof and the editing strategy differ.

## Key Concepts

### The Seniority Spectrum

**Definition**: A continuum of career maturity that determines what counts as an achievement and how it should be framed.

**Levels**:
- **Entry / New-grad**: Little or no full-time paid experience. Evidence comes from school, projects, internships, volunteer, and part-time work.
- **Mid-career**: One+ years of full-time professional experience. Experience leads; education moves below it (unless degree drives a pivot).
- **Senior / Experienced ("mature")**: Long, varied career. The challenge is focus and recency, not finding evidence. Counter age bias with current, results-driven content.

### What Counts as an Achievement (by level)

**Definition**: The unit of proof a candidate offers as evidence of value.

- **New-grad**: Class projects, capstones, research, awards/scholarships, GPA (3.5+), club/team leadership, internships, transferable skills from unrelated jobs.
- **Mid-career**: Delivered results in role — projects shipped, problems solved, measurable outcomes.
- **Senior**: Scope (teams, budgets, regions led), leadership, sustained results, resilience through change ("tower of strength").

### Tower of Strength (Senior)

**Definition**: Demonstrating resilience and value through results — problems solved, costs cut, systems improved, fast adaptation to change.

Employers value professionals who have weathered change and come out stronger. Use outcome examples, not tenure claims, as proof.

### Recency Window (Senior)

**Definition**: Focus on the last ~10-15 years of experience — where employers and ATS look first.

Older high-value roles go under a separate heading (Key/Additional/Prior Experience) with titles, employers, locations — but no dates, or total years only.

### Age-Dating (Senior)

**Definition**: Resume signals that reveal full career span and invite age bias.

Includes graduation years, ancient role dates, decades of detail, outdated standards (marital status, hobbies). Remove them; what you studied matters more than when.

## Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| RC order | Reverse-chronological order (most recent first); the ATS-safe default layout |
| ATS | Applicant Tracking System; parses formatted entries and keyword-matches |
| Data-mining | Reframing every class/project/role to surface marketable, transferable value |
| Transferable skills | Abilities (customer service, problem-solving) that carry across unrelated fields |
| Knowledge, Skills & Training | A 2-3 column keyword block for new grads, placed under the summary |
| Tower of strength | Senior framing: resilience proven through results |
| Recency window | The ~10-15 year span employers and ATS prioritize |
| Top third | Prime resume real estate — lead with strongest content here |

## How It Relates To

- **Keyword/ATS strategy**: Both levels mine target job postings for keywords; new grads use them to fill gaps, seniors to prove currency.
- **Career-change framing**: Pivoting candidates at any level lead with transferable skills and may surface education above experience.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Myth**: New grads have little to offer, so the skills/keyword section can be skipped.
  **Reality**: Course descriptions, syllabi, and project lists supply specific, job-aligned tools and concepts.

- **Myth**: Only paid jobs belong in the Experience section.
  **Reality**: Internships, volunteer, and leadership roles count — formatted like jobs, ATS recognizes them.

- **Myth**: Older workers can't or won't learn new skills.
  **Reality**: The over-50 group is the fastest-growing set of digital learners — cite recent certs, software, and continuing education.

- **Myth**: A longer resume showing a full career is more impressive.
  **Reality**: Relevance beats completeness; trim to recent, on-target content.

## Quick Reference

| Seniority | Evidence source | Framing emphasis | Edit strategy |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------|---------------|
| Entry / new-grad | Projects, internships, volunteer, part-time, GPA, transferable skills | Workplace readiness; results over duties | Add keyword block; lead with education/projects when thin |
| Mid-career | Delivered results in role | Outcomes and impact | Experience first, education below |
| Senior / experienced | Scope, leadership, sustained results | Recent impact; "tower of strength"; currency | Cut to last 10-15 yrs; drop dates/age-dating |
