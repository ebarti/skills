# Achievement Anatomy Rules

Do/don't rules for writing achievement bullets that sell instead of tell.

## Core Rules

### 1. Lead with the result or impact

Put the outcome first; the task is supporting context, not the headline.

- The reader should grasp the payoff before the "how."
- Bad: "Was responsible for staff training, which raised satisfaction by 20%."
- Good: "Raised customer satisfaction 20% by overhauling staff training."

### 2. Sell the outcome, don't just state the task

Every bullet should answer "what changed because of me?" — not merely "what was I assigned?"

- Bad: "Handled inventory management."
- Good: "Cut monthly inventory shrink from 20% to under 5%, saving ~$1,200/month."

### 3. Always quantify when possible

Attach a number, percentage, or dollar figure to the result.

- Pick the lever the data supplies; use more than one if natural.
- A bullet with a figure beats the same bullet without one every time.

### 4. One accomplishment per bullet

Each bullet covers a single challenge→action→result thread.

- Don't chain unrelated wins with "and"; split them.
- Keeps bullets scannable and each achievement distinct.

### 5. Use concrete figures, not vague intensifiers

Replace "many," "significantly," "various," "a lot" with actual counts.

- Bad: "Significantly increased sales." → Good: "Increased sales 32%."
- Bad: "Supervised a large team." → Good: "Supervised a staff of 12."

### 6. Show before/after to frame the change

A result lands harder when paired with the starting point.

- "Improved center ranking from 23rd to 7th out of 27."
- "Lifted average ticket from $350 to $580."

### 7. Start each bullet with a strong action verb

Begin with what you *did* (Increased, Reduced, Launched, Recovered), not "Responsible for" or "Duties included."

## Guidelines

- Build every bullet on a CAR/PAR spine, even if the Challenge stays implicit.
- Mine recommendations, award memos, thank-you emails, and reviews for ready-made proof.
- Keep working notes verbose; cut to succinct, results-first bullets at the final-resume stage.
- Prefer dollars > percentages > raw numbers when more than one figure is available and credible.

## Exceptions

When these rules may be relaxed:

- **Confidential / proprietary figures**: Use ranges ("$1M–$2M"), percentages, or relative terms ("doubled," "top quartile") instead of exact secret numbers.
- **Genuinely no hard metric**: Quantify by frequency, scale, scope, or time saved (e.g., "across 5 sites," "for 200+ clients weekly," "cutting a 3-day process to 1").
- **Pure-duty roles early in career**: A few responsibility bullets are acceptable, but front-load any results you do have.

## Quick Reference

| Rule | Summary |
|------|---------|
| Lead with result | Outcome first, task second |
| Sell not tell | "What changed?" beats "what was assigned?" |
| Quantify | Add #, %, or $ to every result |
| One per bullet | Single challenge→action→result each |
| Concrete figures | Real numbers, not "many"/"significantly" |
| Before/after | Show the starting point and the gain |
| Strong verb start | No "Responsible for" / "Duties included" |
